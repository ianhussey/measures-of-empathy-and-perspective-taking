## Abstract

Relational Frame Theory (RFT) models perspective taking as deictic relational responding, and the deictic relations protocol of Barnes-Holmes (2001) and McHugh, Barnes-Holmes, and Barnes-Holmes (2004) — here the Perspective-Taking Protocol (PTP) — is its standard measure. The protocol is widely used in adult and clinical research to assess perspective-taking ability — to compare clinical and non-clinical groups and to relate deictic performance to other measures — uses that presuppose, but essentially never demonstrate, a reliable and valid measure. We administered a shortened PTP, balanced across simple, reversed, and double-reversed trial types, to 134 adults online and retested 98 of them ten days later, alongside four other measures treated as indices of perspective taking. The PTP had poor internal consistency (α = .40), test–retest reliability indistinguishable from zero (ICC₂,₁ = .08, 95% CI [−.12, .27]), trial-type subscales that did not cohere, and zero convergence with any other perspective-taking measure (all |r| ≤ .08). The same battery reproduced expected, adequate reliabilities for self-report and other performance measures, so the result is specific to the PTP rather than an artifact of the sample or design. We conclude that the PTP, at least in this form, does not measure perspective taking as a stable property of persons. Calling it a perspective-taking measure is a jingle fallacy with real consequences for the RFT literature that relies on it.

**Keywords:** perspective taking; deictic relational responding; relational frame theory; reliability; convergent validity; jingle fallacy

## 1. Introduction

For RFT, perspective taking is not a faculty but a behaviour: arbitrarily applicable relational responding in terms of three *deictic* relations, interpersonal (I–YOU), spatial (HERE–THERE), and temporal (NOW–THEN), elaborated across three levels of relational complexity (simple, reversed, and double-reversed; Hayes, Barnes-Holmes, & Roche, 2001; McHugh et al., 2004). The protocol Barnes-Holmes (2001) devised to assess and train these relations is, in its various slight iterations, the main measurement instrument of this account. It has been used to chart development in children and, in adults, to compare clinical and non-clinical groups — high versus low social anhedonia (Villatte et al., 2008), and anxiety, psychosis, and healthy controls (Hendriks et al., 2016) — and to relate deictic performance to intellectual and language ability (Gore, Barnes-Holmes, & Murphy, 2010), to theory-of-mind tests (Hendriks et al., 2016), and to social anhedonia, empathy, and experiential avoidance (Vilardaga, Estévez, Levin, & Hayes, 2012). The original developmental-profile study itself reported adult (18–30 years) performance (McHugh et al., 2004), related deictic-complexity and false-belief studies have used the same framework with adults (McHugh, Barnes-Holmes, & Barnes-Holmes, 2006, 2007a, 2007b), and adult administrations continue (e.g., in Borderline Personality Disorder; Walton et al., 2024). For a comprehensive review, see Kavanagh, Barnes-Holmes, and Barnes-Holmes (2020).

These latter uses in adults make a strong, usually implicit, psychometric assumption: that the protocol's score is a reliable, valid measure of a perspective-taking individual differences trait. Yet, as Kavanagh et al. (2020) emphasise, the protocol was "explicitly designed for developmental purposes". That is, as a training protocol to establish deictic relations in children where they are absent, not to discriminate stably among adults who already possess this repertoire. The properties a training protocol needs to do the first job (i.e., sensitivity to the presence or absence of a repertoire) are not the properties an individual differences measure needs to do the second (i.e., reliable rank-ordering of individuals who generally possess this repertoire). Whether the protocol has the latter properties has, to our knowledge, never been directly tested. This is despite the fact that, according to a 2017 review (Montoya-Rodríguez et al., 2017), at least 24% of the RFT literature on perspective taking was conducted in typically-developing adult samples where these psychometric properties are necessary for valid conclusions. 

This gap sits within a broader reckoning with measurement across psychology. Alongside the replication crisis, a parallel "measurement crisis" has highlighted how rarely the validity of our measures is interrogated before they are used to draw conclusions (Flake & Fried, 2020). The consequences are not hypothetical: widely used scales have been found to harbour "hidden invalidity" that goes unnoticed precisely because their psychometric properties are assumed rather than checked (Hussey & Hughes, 2020), and the distribution of published reliability estimates itself shows an aberrant abundance of values sitting just above conventional thresholds — a pattern more consistent with selective reporting than with a literature of genuinely reliable instruments (Hussey et al., 2025). Measurement practice also tends toward proliferation rather than consolidation: measures are treated like toothbrushes, with researchers preferring to invent their own rather than reuse and validate shared tools (Elson et al., 2023), multiplying the opportunities for the jingle fallacy — assuming that instruments which share a name thereby share a construct (Thorndike, 1904; Block, 1995). Behavioural tasks, including those used in RFT, have been subjected to markedly less psychometric scrutiny than self-report scales, leaving them especially exposed to these problems; a high-profile recent example is the scrutiny of the Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test, for which researchers now recommend that use be discontinued (Higgins et al., 2024, 2025).

This study therefore tested a shortened version of the Perspective Taking Protocol's psychometric properties. Specifically, its internal concistency, its test-retest reliability, and its convergent validity with other well-established self-report and behavioral measures of perspective taking, theory of mind, and empathy. These broader constructs of theory of of mind an empathy were included on the basis that conceptual RFT work frequently claims that deictic relational responding forms the basis of these other behavioral repertoires (see Kavanagh et al., 2020). 

## 2. Method

Full materials, scoring, analysis code, and the processed data are available at [OSF/GITHUB LINK]. The PTP-relevant details are given below.

### 2.1 Participants

Adults were recruited online via Prolific (prolific.co), with native English and age ≥ 18 as eligibility criteria, and compensated £9 for completing both waves.¹ At Time 1, 134 participants provided usable data (2 excluded for failing embedded attention checks); 98 were retested at Time 2 after a 10-day interval (a further 2 excluded). The Time 1 sample averaged 38.2 years of age (SD = 13.0, range 19–75) and was 67.9% female, 31.3% male, and 0.7% non-binary. Ethical approval was granted by [ETHICS BOARD / REF].

### 2.2 The Perspective-Taking Protocol

We administered a shortened, computer-based, forced-choice version of the McHugh et al. (2004) deictic relations protocol. It comprised 9 trials designed to be balanced across the three trial types — 3 simple, 3 reversed, and 3 double-reversed. One intended double-reversed trial was implemented in the survey as a single reversal and is scored as a reversed trial, giving a final composition of 3 simple, 4 reversed, and 2 double-reversed trials. Each trial was scored correct (1) or incorrect (0); we computed a total score (0–9) and three trial-type subscale scores.

### 2.3 Comparison measures

Participants completed a battery of self-report and performance measures spanning perspective taking, theory of mind, and empathy. Conceptual RFT work treats these as closely related, frequently claiming that deictic relational responding underpins theory of mind and empathy more broadly (Kavanagh et al., 2020); they therefore constitute the relevant field against which to assess the PTP's convergent and discriminant validity.

*Self-report.* The **Interpersonal Reactivity Index** (IRI; Davis, 1983; 28 items), scored as a total and four 7-item subscales — Perspective Taking, Fantasy, Empathic Concern, and Personal Distress. The **Empathy Quotient** (EQ; Baron-Cohen & Wheelwright, 2004; 40 scored items), scored as a total and, following Lawrence et al. (2004), as cognitive-empathy, emotional-reactivity, and social-skills subscales. The **Pictorial Empathy Test** (PET; Lindeman et al., 2018; 7 items), indexing affective empathy. The **Single Item Trait Empathy Scale** (SITES; Konrath et al., 2018), a single-item global empathy measure.

*Performance.* The **Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test** (RMET; Baron-Cohen et al., 2001; 36 items), a test of mental-state inference (theory of mind). The **Perspective-Taking Task for Adults** (PTT-A; [CITATION]; 32 items), a performance measure of perspective taking.

The measures most directly comparable to the PTP as indices of *perspective taking* are the IRI Perspective-Taking subscale and the EQ cognitive-empathy subscale (self-report), and the RMET and PTT-A (performance); these constitute the primary convergent-validity set, with the remaining empathy (sub)scales providing discriminant context.

### 2.4 Analysis

Internal consistency was estimated as Cronbach's α (raw α with Feldt 95% CIs; `psych::alpha`) on Time 1 data. Test–retest reliability was estimated as the intraclass correlation coefficient — two-way model, absolute agreement, single measurement (ICC₂,₁; `irr::icc`) — following Koo and Li (2016). Convergent validity was indexed by Pearson correlations among Time 1 scores; we report both uncorrected and Holm-corrected *p*-values. To estimate the relationship between the measures' *true* (temporally stable) scores, we also disattenuated each correlation for measurement unreliability, dividing it by the square root of the product of the two measures' test–retest reliabilities, $r_{xy}^{\text{corrected}} = r_{xy} / \sqrt{ICC_x \cdot ICC_y}$. Finally, to summarise the convergent-validity pattern, we treated each correlation as an effect size (Fisher r-to-z transformed, sampling variance $1/(n-3)$) in a mixed-effects meta-analysis (`metafor`) with a binary moderator contrasting correlations that involve the PTP with those among non-PTP measures, after excluding within-instrument (part–whole) correlations; standard errors and confidence intervals were cluster-robust, and pooled estimates are reported back-transformed to Pearson's *r*. Analyses were run in R [VERSION]; code reproducing every value and figure is in the repository (`code/analysis_ptp.Rmd`).

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Reliability

The PTP failed both reliability criteria, and failed them alone (Table 1; Figure 1). Its internal consistency was poor (α = .40) and its test–retest reliability was statistically indistinguishable from zero (ICC₂,₁ = .08, 95% CI [−.12, .27]) — that is, a participant's PTP score at Time 1 carried essentially no information about their score ten days later. None of the three trial-type subscales reached ICC₂,₁ = .35. Every comparison measure, by contrast, reached conventionally acceptable-to-good reliability on both indices, including the two *performance* tasks (RMET, PTT-A). Low PTP reliability is therefore not a generic property of brief tasks or of this sample; it is specific to the PTP.

**Figure 1.** Reliability of the PTP (total and trial-type subscales) and comparison measures. Points are estimates and horizontal ranges are 95% confidence intervals; the dotted line marks zero, the dashed line marks a common acceptability threshold of .70. The PTP and its subscales sit far below every other measure on both indices.

### 3.2 Convergent validity

The PTP total score was uncorrelated with every other perspective-taking measure: IRI-PT (*r* = −.08), EQ-Cog (*r* = −.04), RMET (*r* = .03), and PTT-A (*r* = −.02). None approached significance even before correction (uncorrected *p* = .37, .68, .75, and .78, respectively; Holm-corrected *p* = 1.00 throughout; Figure 2). The protocol was also internally incoherent: its three trial-type subscales were mutually independent (simple–reversed *r* = .22; simple–double-reversed *r* = .02; reversed–double-reversed *r* = −.08). The subscales' correlations with the PTP total (*r* = .50–.76) are part–whole artifacts and are not evidence of convergent validity.

The same pattern held across the *full* battery, not just the perspective-taking subset: the PTP total correlated near zero with every other (sub)scale administered — empathy, theory-of-mind, and perspective-taking measures alike — with no correlation exceeding |*r*| = .11 and none significant even before correction (all uncorrected *p* ≥ .19; Figure 2, top). For context, the only appreciable convergence among the perspective-taking measures was between the two *self-report* scales (IRI-PT and EQ-Cog, *r* = .44, *p* < .001); the performance measures correlated only weakly with the self-reports and with one another (all |*r*| ≤ .17). Convergence across this domain is weak in general — but the PTP is the one measure that converges with nothing at all.

Disattenuating these correlations for measurement unreliability does not change the conclusion. Correcting each PTP–measure correlation by the test–retest reliabilities of the two measures (i.e., estimating the correlation between their temporally stable components) leaves the PTP essentially unrelated to the other perspective-taking measures: IRI-PT *r* = −.31, EQ-Cog *r* = −.14, RMET *r* = .12, and PTT-A *r* = −.11 — at most 9% shared variance, and only with a self-report subscale. These corrected values are upper bounds and are themselves highly unstable here, because dividing by the PTP's near-zero test–retest reliability (ICC₂,₁ = .08) inflates them substantially; the more robust reading is simply that there is too little reliable PTP variance for any true correlation to be recovered. By the same correction, the one genuine convergence in the set — the two self-report scales — strengthens as expected (IRI-PT with EQ-Cog, *r* = .53). The full observed and disattenuated matrices appear in Figure 2.

![Observed and disattenuated correlations among all measures](../code/plots/cor_all_disattenuated_t1.png)

**Figure 2.** Correlations among all (sub)scales at Time 1: (top) observed Pearson correlations; (bottom) the same correlations disattenuated for each pair's test–retest reliability. Disattenuated values are clamped to [−1, 1] for display; out-of-range cells (the PTP part–whole pairs, inflated by the PTP's near-zero reliability) are reported exactly in the repository tables. The PTP (top-left block) remains near zero with every other measure in both panels.

A moderator meta-analysis with cluster-robust standard errors formalised this contrast. Across the *k* = 114 cross-instrument correlations (52 involving the PTP, 62 among non-PTP measures, excluding within-scale/subscale correlations), correlations involving the PTP or its subscales with the contrast measures pooled at an average *r* = .01, 95% CI [−.01, .04], compared to correlations among the contrast measures having an average *r* = .28 [.19, .37] (moderator *p* < .001). Applying the same analysis to the correlations after disattenuating for test–retest reliability (therefore controlling for scale length, etc) did not narrow the gap: PTP-contrast correlations *r* = .03 [−.04, .10] versus contrast-contrast correlations*r* = .35 [.23, .47] (moderator *p* < .001). Because the RMET itself has poor and contested psychometric properties (Higgins et al., 2023, 2024), as a sensitivity analysis we repeated the meta-analysis excluding every correlation involving it (*k* = 98). Very similar results were observed: contrast-contrast correlations *r* = .31 [.20, .41] and disattentuated correlations *r* = .39 [.25, .52], versus PTP-contrast correlations *r* = .01 [−.01, .04] and disattenutated correlations *r* = .03 [−.05, .11]; both moderator *p*s < .001. 

## 4. Discussion

A measure used to rank people on perspective taking should rank them consistently and should agree with other measures of the same thing. The shortened PTP did neither: poor internal consistency, test–retest reliability near zero, trial-type subscales that do not cohere, and no shared variance with four other perspective-taking measures. The most parsimonious reading is that PTP scores are dominated by occasion-specific error rather than any stable property of the person, and that whatever residual signal they contain is unrelated to what other "perspective-taking" instruments capture. Using the name "perspective taking" for this score is, on the present evidence, a jingle fallacy.

This is not a wholesale indictment of the RFT account of perspective taking, nor of the deictic-relations analysis. It is an indictment of treating a developmental assessment-and-training protocol as an individual-differences instrument — precisely the slippage Kavanagh et al. (2020) flagged. Among competent adults, ceiling and floor effects within trial types, heterogeneity across the three deictic relations, and very small trial counts leave little reliable between-person variance for a total score to capture. That a *different* performance task in the same battery (the PTT-A) was reliable shows the problem is the instrument, not the modality.

The PTP is not the only widely used behavioural measure of a social-cognitive construct to founder under psychometric scrutiny, and the most directly comparable case is instructive. The Reading the Mind in the Eyes Test (RMET) — included here as a theory-of-mind benchmark, and itself converging only weakly with the other measures (all |*r*| ≤ .17) — has recently been examined in exactly this way. In a large, demographically representative sample its factor structure fit poorly and its internal consistency was weak (Higgins, Ross, Langdon, & Polito, 2023); a systematic review of 1,461 studies found that 63% reported no validity evidence whatsoever, prompting the conclusion that RMET scores, and the literature built on them, are "largely unsubstantiated and uninterpretable" (Higgins, Kaplan, Deschrijver, & Ross, 2024); and the authors have since reiterated their recommendation that researchers stop using the test (Higgins, Kaplan, Deschrijver, & Ross, 2025). The parallel sets a useful benchmark. The RMET has attracted far more validation effort than the PTP and is nonetheless judged unfit for purpose — yet on the present data the PTP is *less* reliable still (α = .40 vs. .68; ICC₂,₁ = .08 vs. .73) while sharing the same near-total absence of convergent validity. If the evidentiary standard that warrants retiring the RMET is the right one, the PTP does not meet it either.

### Consequences for the literature

Reliability near zero severely attenuates and destabilises any correlation involving PTP scores. Reports that relate PTP performance to clinical status or other constructs are therefore difficult to interpret: true associations are masked, and null associations are uninformative. At a minimum, studies using the protocol as a covariate, outcome, or moderator should report its reliability in their own sample and propagate that uncertainty into their inferences.

### Recommendations

(1) Reliability and convergent validity should be reported as a precondition for substantive use of the PTP, not assumed. (2) The three deictic relations may be better treated as distinct response classes than collapsed into a single perspective-taking score. (3) The field would benefit from an instrument purpose-built — and validated — for individual differences in deictic relational responding, rather than the continued repurposing of a developmental protocol.

### Limitations

We tested a *shortened, balanced* PTP; a longer protocol with more trials per relation might improve reliability (though it is harder to see how it would rescue the convergent-validity null). The sample was a non-clinical, native-English online panel, and the picture may differ in the developmental and clinical populations for which the protocol was designed. PTT-A scoring currently keys each item to its modal response and should be confirmed against the original scheme, and one PTP trial was mis-implemented as noted above. [ANY FURTHER LIMITATIONS.]

### Conclusion

This shortened version of the Perspective-Taking Protocol appears to suffer from the jingle fallacy: naming it a measure of perspective taking does not make it so. Results demonstrated that it has near-zero convergent validity with other measures of perspective taking, theory of mind, or empathy. The Perspective-Taking Protocol was also shown to be extremely unreliable: its internal consistency is low and its test–retest reliability is near zero. It is possible that a longer version of the task would demonstrate higher reliability, given that reliability is a function of task length; however, increasing task length alone is unlikely to produce a reliable and valid measure. Applying the Spearman–Brown prophecy formula to the observed internal consistency (α = .40 over 9 trials) shows that a full-length protocol could indeed be internally consistent: the 62-trial version administered to adults by McHugh et al. (2004), Villatte et al. (2008), and Hendriks et al. (2016) projects to α ≈ .82, and the 256-trial original (Barnes-Holmes, 2001) to α ≈ .95 (reaching α = .80 requires ~54 trials; α = .90, ~122). Two caveats blunt this apparent reassurance. First, the projection assumes the added trials are parallel indicators of the same true score. Second, and more importantly, it speaks *only* to internal consistency: it does not address the near-zero test–retest reliability — correcting the observed 10-day stability for internal unreliability implies a true stability of only ~.20, so even the 62-trial protocol would be expected to yield a test–retest reliability of just ~.16 — nor the absent convergent validity (which lengthening cannot remedy at all; even disattenuated for unreliability, the PTP's correlations with other perspective-taking measures remain weak and unstable, at most ~9% shared variance; §3.2). A longer protocol could thus be internally consistent while still measuring something that is unstable across occasions and unrelated to every other perspective-taking measure. We therefore suggest that the conclusions reached here are likely to generalize to the longer versions of the Perspective-Taking Protocol used in other research (noting that task length varies across existing studies, from 34 to 62 trials in adult samples; see Appendix A). In this study the McHugh PTP was unreliable and shared no variance with other perspective-taking measures, and so cannot bear the individual-differences inferences routinely placed on it.
